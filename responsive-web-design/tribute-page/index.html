Marie Curie: A Pioneer in Science

Marie Skłodowska Curie was a Polish and naturalized-French physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, the first person and only woman to win the Nobel Prize twice, and the only person to win a Nobel Prize in two different scientific fields.

Timeline

  • 1867: Born Maria Skłodowska on November 7 in Warsaw, Poland.
  • 1891: Moved to Paris to attend the Sorbonne and pursue studies in physics and mathematics.
  • 1893: Completed her master's degree in physics, ranked first in her class.
  • 1894: Earned a second degree in mathematics from the Sorbonne. Met Pierre Curie, a French physicist. They married in 1895.
  • 1896: Began research on uranium rays, inspired by Henri Becquerel's discovery of radioactivity.
  • 1898: Discovered the elements polonium (named after her native Poland) and radium with Pierre Curie.
  • 1903: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics along with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for their work on radioactivity, making her the first woman to win a Nobel Prize.
  • 1906: Pierre Curie died in a tragic accident. Marie Curie took over his teaching position at the Sorbonne, becoming the first woman to teach there.
  • 1911: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her discovery of radium and polonium, making her the first person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields.
  • 1914: Established the Radium Institute (now Curie Institute) in Paris, which became a major center for medical research.
  • 1914-1918: During World War I, developed mobile radiography units, known as "Little Curies," to assist battlefield surgeons with X-ray equipment.
  • 1920s: Continued her research and worked on promoting the use of radium for medical purposes. Made several international trips to raise funds for her research.
  • 1934: Died on July 4 in Sancellemoz Sanatorium, France, from aplastic anemia, believed to be caused by prolonged exposure to radiation.
